Abstract

We develop a geometric theory of quantum entanglement in Φ-Wave Theory. Rather than treating entanglement as a primitive tensor-product phenomenon, we show that it arises from the nonfactorizable global structure of the admissible phase configuration space. A quantum state is entangled when its wavefunctional over phase configurations cannot be decomposed into independent factors associated with subsystems. We define entanglement geometrically in terms of the topology, holonomy, and admissibility structure of the global phase field, derive reduced-state structure from partial restriction of phase configurations, and reinterpret nonlocal correlations as consequences of globally constrained phase geometry. This establishes entanglement as a property of the geometry of \mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{phys}}, not as an added quantum mystery.

3. Definition of Entanglement

Definition 3.1 — Phase Entanglement

A quantum phase state is unentangled if it can be written as

\Psi[\Phi_A,\Phi_B] = \Psi_A[\Phi_A]\Psi_B[\Phi_B].

It is entangled if no such factorization exists.

Thus:

\boxed{
\text{Entanglement = nonfactorizability of the global phase wavefunctional.}
}

This is the direct Phase-native generalization of ordinary quantum entanglement.

4. Why Factorization Fails

Factorization can fail for several distinct reasons in Φ-Wave Theory:

4.1 Dynamical Coupling

The action contains interaction terms coupling \Phi_A and \Phi_B.

4.2 Topological Coupling

The total configuration may carry a topological invariant not decomposable into independent subsystem invariants.

4.3 Admissibility Coupling

The admissibility condition may constrain the total phase geometry globally:

\mathcal{A}[\Phi_A,\Phi_B] = 0

without decomposing into independent subsystem conditions.

4.4 Holonomy Coupling

Global phase transport around loops involving both subsystems may define observables not attributable to either subsystem alone.

Thus entanglement is not a single mechanism but a generic consequence of global phase structure.

7. Reduced States and Partial Phase Restriction

To define a reduced state for subsystem A, we integrate over the subsystem B degrees of freedom:

\rho_A[\Phi_A,\Phi_A']

\int \mathcal{D}\mu_B\,
\Psi[\Phi_A,\Phi_B]\Psi^*[\Phi_A',\Phi_B].

This defines the reduced density functional of A.

If the total state factorizes, then \rho_A is pure.
If not, \rho_A is mixed.

Thus standard reduced-state structure is recovered, but now interpreted geometrically in terms of partial restriction of the global phase configuration.

17. Entanglement Geometry Theorem

Theorem 17.1

A composite quantum phase state is entangled if and only if its admissible wavefunctional cannot be factorized into independent subsystem functionals. Equivalently, entanglement occurs whenever the admissible joint configuration space is not geometrically separable into independent subsystem phase spaces.

Proof Sketch

1. A quantum phase state is defined as a wavefunctional on the admissible joint configuration space.
2. If this space factorizes and the wavefunctional factorizes, the state is separable.
3. If the admissible constraints, topology, or holonomy structure couple the subsystems, the joint space is not separable.
4. In that case the wavefunctional cannot generally be written as a product, and the state is entangled.

∎
