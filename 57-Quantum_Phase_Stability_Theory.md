Persistence, Decay, and Dynamical Selection of Phase Configurations

Abstract

We develop a comprehensive theory of stability in Φ-Wave Theory, unifying classical stability, topological protection, admissibility constraints, and quantum decay processes. We classify phase configurations according to dynamical, energetic, topological, and quantum stability criteria, and derive conditions under which excitations, vacuum sectors, and geometric structures persist or decay. We show that stability is governed by the interplay of curvature energy, admissibility, renormalization flow, and nonperturbative tunneling amplitudes. This establishes a hierarchy of stability—ranging from absolutely stable topological sectors to metastable configurations subject to instanton-mediated decay—and provides the selection principle for physically realized phase structures.

2. Definition of Stability

Definition 2.1 — Quantum Phase Stability

A phase configuration \Phi_0 is quantum-stable if:

1. it satisfies admissibility: \mathcal{A}[\Phi_0]=0,
2. small perturbations do not grow dynamically,
3. no allowed finite-action tunneling path leads to rapid decay,
4. it remains stable under RG flow at relevant scales.

Thus:

\boxed{
\text{stability = persistence under dynamics + admissibility + quantum fluctuations}
}
