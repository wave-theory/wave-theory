Abstract

We develop the theory of phase boundaries and interface geometry in Φ-Wave Theory, describing regions where the phase field \Phi : M \to \mathcal{M} transitions between distinct vacuum sectors or topological configurations. We show that interfaces are geometric objects characterized by gradients, curvature discontinuities, and localized energy densities. We derive interface equations from the Parent Φ-Wave Action, classify boundary types using homotopy and cohomology, and demonstrate that interfaces naturally support localized modes, flux trapping, and reduced-dimensional dynamics. This paper establishes interfaces as fundamental physical structures where bulk phase topology, energy, and admissibility constraints intersect.

2. Definition of a Phase Boundary

Let two regions U_1, U_2 \subset M have:

\Phi(x) \in \mathcal{M}_1 \quad \text{for } x \in U_1
\Phi(x) \in \mathcal{M}_2 \quad \text{for } x \in U_2

A phase boundary is the interface:

\Sigma = \partial U_1 \cap \partial U_2

across which \Phi transitions.
